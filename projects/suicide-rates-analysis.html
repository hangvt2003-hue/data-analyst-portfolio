Suicide Rates Across Countries

A 35+ year cross-country dataset analyzed with K-Means clustering. Looking at the patterns where economic conditions, cultural dimensions, and demographic structure intersect with national mental health outcomes.

01 — A note on the topic

Difficult subject matter, treated carefully.

This is a sensitive topic, and the analysis is approached as a public-health and social-science exercise — not a casual data exploration. The goal is to understand the macroscale structure of national suicide rates and how they correlate with broader social, economic, and cultural variables — work that supports public-health policy discussion, not individual judgments.

If you or someone you know is struggling — please reach out to local support resources. In Vietnam, contact the National Mental Health Hotline. International readers: see findahelpline.com for region-specific support.
02 — Approach

From cleaning to clustering.

1
Data integration
Combined WHO suicide-rate data with World Bank economic indicators and Hofstede's cultural-dimension scores. The merge required careful country-name normalization and handling of countries that don't appear in all sources.
2
Exploratory analysis
Time-series patterns across decades, age-and-sex-stratified rates, regional clustering, and the relationship between economic shocks (recessions, currency crises) and short-term rate changes.
3
K-Means clustering
Clustered countries based on a feature vector combining mean rate, age-distribution shape, sex ratio, and cultural dimensions. Used the elbow method and silhouette score to choose k. The resulting clusters had clear interpretations — high-developed-country cluster, post-Soviet cluster, etc.
4
Interpretation and reporting
Wrote up findings with the awareness that statistical correlation in this domain has policy implications — careful framing of what the data does and does not tell us.
