Customer Behavior at Supermarket

An exploratory analysis of supermarket sensor data to inform store layout decisions. Foot-traffic heatmaps, shelf-level conversion funnels, demographic segmentation, and the dwell-time–purchase paradox.

Context
Academic project · NEU
Year
2024
My Role
Lead Analyst
Stack
Power BI · Excel · Statistics
01 — The brief

Why is the bakery aisle drawing crowds but no purchases?

The dataset captured anonymized shopper trajectories — entry, dwell time per zone, and final purchases — for a mid-size supermarket over 8 weeks. The store manager wanted to know: which zones are doing their job, and which are just collecting bodies?

"Foot traffic" is an attractive metric because it's easy to visualize, but it's a vanity metric on its own. What matters is foot traffic that converts — and the relationship is far from linear.

The interesting finding upfront

Some of the busiest zones had the lowest conversion. The bakery zone, in particular, had 2× the dwell time of the produce zone but ½ the conversion rate. The answer was a layout choice — and it was actionable.

02 — Approach

From sensor data to layout recommendations.

1
Data cleaning and zone definition
Cleaned trajectory data — duplicate timestamps, sensor gaps, outlier dwell times — and defined logical zones (produce, bakery, dairy, frozen, checkout, etc.) on the store layout grid.
2
Heatmaps and traffic patterns
Built spatial heatmaps showing foot-traffic density by hour of day. Surfaced the predictable patterns (lunch-rush deli queue) and the surprising ones (a dead corner near frozen foods that customers actively avoided).
3
Conversion funnels at the zone level
For each zone: customers who entered → customers who lingered >30 sec → customers who took an item → customers who paid for an item from that zone. The funnel exposed the bakery paradox (high dwell, low conversion) and the produce success (moderate dwell, high conversion).
4
Demographic segmentation
Cross-tabbed traffic patterns by inferred customer demographic (time of visit, basket composition). Different segments traversed the store in different ways — implying that any layout change has segment-specific impact.
5
Layout recommendations with reasoning
Concrete, prioritized recommendations: relocate the bakery display, add a signed shortcut from frozen to checkout, and rearrange the impulse-purchase rack at checkout based on the actual queue patterns.

04 — What this showcases

Skills demonstrated.

Spatial reasoning

Layout-aware data work

Most retail analytics is purely transactional. Adding the spatial dimension reveals the upstream "why" behind transaction patterns.

Funnel discipline

Beyond traffic counting

Foot-traffic alone is a vanity metric. The right unit of analysis is conversion at each zone — and the funnel structure makes that explicit.

Cross-domain transfer

Same patterns, different domain

The bakery-conversion problem mirrors the "high-dwell low-conversion level" problem in mobile games — same diagnostic logic, different domain.

Actionable output

Recommendations, not just findings

The deliverable ended with concrete layout changes ranked by expected impact and effort — not a list of charts the reader is left to interpret.
