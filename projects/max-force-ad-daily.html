Choosing the Optimal Daily Force-Ad Cap

When does an extra forced ad start hurting more than it helps? Two complementary methodologies — a behavioral-percentile approach and an LTV-curve approach — converge on the same answer from very different angles.

Context
Mobile Gaming Studio · Multiple titles
Year

My Role

01 — The problem

Forced ads are a sharp knife.

In F2P mobile games, forced ads — interstitials and AOA (App Open Ads) — are the involuntary monetization layer. Unlike rewarded video, the user can't opt out. Show too few, and you leave money on the table. Show too many, and you erode session quality, increase early-day churn, and corrode the lifetime value (LTV) curve.

The Project Owner asked a deceptively simple question: what is the optimal daily cap? The naive answer — "test a bunch of caps via A/B and pick the highest revenue one" — fails because LTV manifests over weeks, not days, and most A/B tests are run on day-7 metrics.

The reframe

Instead of asking "what's the optimal cap?", I split the question into two: (a) what's the natural distribution of ad-tolerance across users? and (b) at what cap does each additional ad start destroying more LTV than it earns?

02 — Two methodologies, side by side

Behavioral percentile vs. LTV-curve.

I researched two methodologies and ran both. They start from completely different premises but produced caps within 1–2 ads of each other for every game studied — which is the kind of cross-validation that makes a recommendation robust.

Method 1
Behavioral Percentile

Premise: the existing user base is itself the experiment. Look at the distribution of forced ads served per active day, then pick a percentile that balances revenue capture vs. user discomfort.

  • Compute distribution of force_ads_served_per_active_day across the active user base over the trailing 30 days.
  • Identify the P75–P85 region as the natural soft ceiling — the cap above which only the highest-tolerance users sit.
  • Validate the candidate cap doesn't truncate any healthy retention cohort by cross-tabbing with D7/D30 retention.

Strengths: easy to communicate, fast, no causal modeling needed. Limits: doesn't directly measure LTV impact — only reveals the natural shape.

Method 2
LTV-Curve Marginal Analysis

Premise: the right cap is where the marginal revenue from one more forced ad equals the marginal LTV destruction.

  • Bucket users by their actual avg_force_ads_per_day in the first 7 days.
  • Plot D30 LTV (IAP + ad revenue) for each bucket. The curve typically rises and then plateaus or inverts.
  • Use the inflection point — where marginal LTV gain ≤ 0 — as the upper-bound recommendation.
  • Critically, control for self-selection — high-tolerance users naturally see more ads, so adjust by retention-matched cohort.

Strengths: directly answers the LTV question. Limits: requires several weeks of data and careful causal hygiene.

Convergence as validation

The two methods don't share assumptions, so when they agree it's a strong signal. For the games I studied, the percentile method's recommendation sat within ±1 ad of the LTV-curve inflection point. That convergence let me ship a recommendation with high confidence — and a clear story for skeptics on either methodological side.

03 — The recommendation

A cap that's both empirical and defensible.

The final recommendation was delivered as a one-page memo to the Project Owner with three components:

  1. Headline number — the recommended daily cap, with a tight confidence range.
  2. Both methodologies, briefly — so the recommendation is reproducible and challengeable.
  3. Monitoring plan — which downstream metrics (D7 retention, ARPDAU, ad opt-in) to watch in the first two weeks after the change, and what would trigger a rollback.
Why this matters

A single-method answer to a high-stakes monetization question is fragile. Two methods that agree are a strong signal. Two methods that disagree are an even more useful signal — they surface where the assumptions are doing the work.

04 — What this showcases

Skills demonstrated.

Methodological rigor

Cross-validation by design

Running two independent methodologies isn't redundancy — it's how you separate "the answer" from "what one method happens to say."

Causal thinking

Self-selection awareness

High ad-tolerance users naturally see more ads. Naive cohort comparison would conflate willingness-to-tolerate with willingness-to-spend. The methodology corrects for this.

Communication

Memo, not deck

Stakeholders read better than they listen. A one-page memo with the answer up top, methods below, and a monitoring plan attached is the format that gets decisions made.

Operational thinking

Built-in rollback criteria

Every recommendation comes with a "what would change my mind" clause — the monitoring plan and the metric thresholds that trigger reconsideration.
