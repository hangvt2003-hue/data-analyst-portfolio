IAP Price Localization

A $4.99 starter pack means very different things in San Francisco, São Paulo, and Hà Nội. Building a defensible region-by-region IAP pricing strategy that combines purchasing-power parity, competitor benchmarks, and historical conversion.

01 — The problem

One global price equals one global price.

Mobile games default to USD-anchored pricing — $0.99, $4.99, $9.99 — that gets converted by the app stores into local-currency tiers. The result: a starter pack costing 1.5 hours of average wages in the US can cost 8+ hours of average wages in lower-income markets. Conversion rates collapse, and users in those markets self-select out of the IAP economy entirely.

The Project Owner asked: can we set region-specific IAP price tiers that capture more revenue from emerging markets without cannibalizing established markets?

The honest framing

This isn't pure benevolence — region-aware pricing increases total revenue by capturing users who would otherwise never buy. The pricing-justice argument and the revenue argument point the same direction.

02 — Approach

Three signals, weighted and combined.

No single signal is enough to set prices. PPP alone over-discounts mature markets. Competitor benchmarks alone propagate the industry's collective mistakes. Historical conversion alone can't distinguish "low conversion because price is wrong" from "low conversion because the market just doesn't spend on mobile games." I built a method that triangulates all three.

1
Build a per-country PPP-adjusted baseline
Pull World Bank PPP indices and compute a "local-currency equivalent" for each USD price tier. This is the floor — any price set higher than this is asking users to pay more than US users in real terms.
2
Layer in competitor benchmarks
Sampled the top 10 games in the same genre in each target country. Recorded their starter-pack and mid-tier prices. This calibrates against actual willingness-to-pay norms in each market — including factors PPP misses (mobile gaming culture, payment infrastructure).
3
Validate against historical conversion data
For markets where we already had IAP data, plot conversion rate vs. price-paid-as-%-of-local-monthly-wage. Use the curve to identify whether current prices are above or below the conversion sweet spot per region.
4
Synthesize and stage
Recommended price tiers per market with explicit reasoning: "For Vietnam, drop starter pack to local equivalent of $2.50; PPP-justified, matches genre median, expected ~2× conversion lift based on the historical curve." Staged rollout with monitoring.

03 — The deliverable

Per-market price recommendations with reasoning.

The output was a structured spreadsheet plus a written memo:

  • Top markets by user count, ranked.
  • For each market: PPP-adjusted price, competitor median, current price, recommended price, expected conversion lift.
  • A "reasoning column" on every row — not just numbers, but the one-sentence justification for the recommendation. This was the critical piece for stakeholder buy-in.
  • Risk flags for markets where the three signals disagreed strongly (e.g., PPP says cheaper, competitors say more expensive — usually indicates weak competitor sample).
Why "reasoning column"

If a stakeholder pushes back on a single market recommendation, you need to explain that one row without re-explaining the entire methodology. Embedding the why next to the what made the spreadsheet self-defending.

04 — What this showcases

Skills demonstrated.

Multi-source synthesis

Three signals, one recommendation

Real pricing decisions require triangulation. PPP, competitive benchmarks, and historical conversion each have blind spots; together they constrain the answer.

External data integration

Beyond first-party data

The work required pulling World Bank PPP data, scraping store rankings, and merging with internal conversion data — comfort with both.

Stakeholder craft

Self-defending deliverables

Per-row reasoning made the spreadsheet survive partial pushback. A market-specific challenge could be answered without re-explaining the whole framework.

Commercial intuition

Pricing-justice meets revenue

Framing the work as both ethically defensible and revenue-positive made it easier to get organizational buy-in than purely commercial framing alone.
