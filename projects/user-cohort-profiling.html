01 — Why the average is misleading

Two games, identical ARPDAU. Completely different futures.

Imagine two games with the same daily ARPDAU. Game A's revenue comes from a long thin tail of casual spenders. Game B's revenue comes from a tiny cluster of high-LTV whales. The same headline metric — but one game's revenue collapses if 10 users churn, while the other can lose hundreds of users without flinching.

Without segmentation, the studio reacts to the same KPIs the same way. With segmentation, the studio learns which users actually drive the headline, and where intervention is most leveraged.

The goal of this work

Build a behavioral cohort profile that the Project Owner and Game Designers could use as standard segments across all subsequent analyses — so every retention or monetization conversation immediately includes "in which segment?"

02 — Approach

Two-axis segmentation: engagement × monetization.

I avoided unsupervised clustering for the primary segmentation. Reason: clusters discovered by k-means or DBSCAN are mathematically clean but stakeholder-illegible. A Game Designer can act on "loyal whale" — they can't act on "Cluster 3, the one with high silhouette score on PC2."

Instead, I used a two-axis behavioral grid with explicit, defensible thresholds, validated against k-means as a sanity check.

1
Define the two axes
Engagement axis: active days in last 30 days × average session length. Monetization axis: 30-day spend (IAP) + 30-day rewarded-ad views (proxy for ad revenue contribution).
2
Set thresholds at natural distribution breaks
Use percentile breaks (P50, P90) on each axis rather than arbitrary numbers. Each segment then has a clear definition: "top 10% by engagement and top 10% by monetization" = whales.
3
Profile each segment behaviorally
For each segment, compute D30 retention, content reach (max level reached), feature usage (events fired), and most-frequent churn-precursor signals.
4
Validate against unsupervised k-means
Run k-means on the same feature set as a sanity check. Confirm the rule-based segments and the algorithmic clusters substantially overlap — gives confidence the boundaries reflect real behavioral structure.

03 — The output

A segment dictionary the team actually uses.

The deliverable was a Tableau dashboard plus a written segment dictionary. Each segment got:

  • A named identity — Loyal Whales, Casual Churners, Dormant Returnees, Steady Mids, etc.
  • A one-sentence behavioral profile — what they do, what they don't.
  • Their share of DAU and revenue — so it's immediately clear which segments deserve outsized attention.
  • Common precursor signals — what changes about their behavior 7 days before they churn.
  • Suggested interventions — content, pricing, or notification strategies that fit the segment's behavior.
Outcome

The segments became the default lens for subsequent conversations. New analyses now arrive pre-segmented. Game Designers reference them in feature briefs. The "average user" framing has measurably retreated from team meetings.

04 — What this showcases

Skills demonstrated.

Methodological choice

Rules over clusters — when stakeholders matter

Choosing rule-based segmentation over unsupervised clustering was a deliberate design decision: stakeholder legibility > mathematical elegance.

Validation discipline

Cross-checking with k-means

Even when not using clustering as the primary tool, I used it as a sanity check — making sure rule-based boundaries weren't completely orthogonal to the actual data structure.

Stakeholder craft

Naming matters

"Loyal Whales" vs. "Segment 4" is the difference between a framework people internalize and one they ignore. Naming was a deliberate part of the deliverable.

Domain understanding

F2P revenue concentration

Knowing that mobile game revenue is heavily skewed toward small high-LTV cohorts shaped the choice of monetization-axis weighting and the focus on whale retention.
