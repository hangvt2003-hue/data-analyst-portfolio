Why Engagement Drops at Specific Levels

A diagnostic funnel investigation on a match-3 puzzle game. Engagement metrics fell off a cliff at certain levels — but daily KPIs alone couldn't tell us which levels, or why. This is how we found the culprits.

01 — The problem

Daily metrics moved together. The story didn't.

The puzzle game we'll call Puzzle Game A showed declining engagement in its weekly report — sessions, time spent, and progression all trending down. The Project Owner asked the obvious question: where exactly is the player base falling off?

Looking at retention curves at the cohort level wasn't enough. Players were technically retained — but disengaged. The question wasn't "are they leaving?" but "are they getting stuck?" — and if so, at which levels and why.

02 — Approach

A four-layer diagnostic funnel.

Rather than picking one metric, I built a layered diagnostic that moved from "is this real?" to "why is this happening?" Each layer answered one question and decided whether to continue digging.

1
Confirm the symptom — D0–D5 trends
Average session length, matches/day, and levels-cleared/day across day-since-install. Compared against two sibling games (Puzzle Game B and Puzzle Game C) to rule out studio-wide or seasonal effects.
2
Localize the drop — per-level baseline win rate
Computed first-attempt win rate per level (1–50), normalized to remove the natural difficulty curve. The chart isolated several specific levels with abnormally low win rates — the suspect levels.
3
Behavior at suspect levels — hint usage & retries
For each suspect level, looked at hint balance, consecutive-fail rate, and time-on-level distribution. A flat hint balance + high consecutive failure was the signature of "frustration without escape valve."
4
Downstream impact — post-failure retention
Modeled the % of users still playing N levels after exposure to a suspect level. Confirmed the suspect levels weren't just hard — they were causing measurable churn into the next 5–10 levels.

04 — What this showcases

Skills demonstrated.

Diagnostic mindset

From symptom to root cause

The investigation didn't stop at "engagement is down." It progressively narrowed: which days, which levels, which behavior, what downstream impact.

Comparative analysis

Sibling-game baselines

Using sister titles as a control isolates game-specific issues from studio-wide or seasonal noise — a small but critical step.

Cross-functional output

Designed for game designers

The report's audience was Game Designers, not analysts. Charts are annotated with thresholds, danger bands, and plain-language conclusions.

Tooling
